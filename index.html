About

I am a postdoctoral fellow at the Center for Brain Science at Harvard University, working at the intersection of computational neuroscience, artificial intelligence and language. My research uses intracranial recordings and modern AI models to study how the human brain encodes structure and meaning during natural language use. I recently completed my Ph.D. in Biophysics at Harvard, where I focused on robust, multimodal neural signals for language in human patients.

Before that, I studied at BITS Pilani, where I spent most of my time oscillating between physics, mathematics and computer science. I worked on problems ranging from gravitational theory and geometry to neural networks, fuzzy logic and augmented reality for safer surgeries, supported in part by a General Electric Foundation Fellowship. Somewhere between those projects, I became interested in how abstract computation and everyday cognition meet in the brain, and I have been chasing that question ever since.

Research

My work asks how intelligent computation emerges in biological and artificial systems, with a particular focus on language. On the biological side, I study large-scale intracranial recordings during naturalistic language and multimodal tasks to understand how the brain represents parts of speech, syntax and semantic composition across space and time. On the artificial side, I use recurrent networks and modern language models as tools to probe how formal structure, compositionality and hierarchy arise from learning.

I am especially interested in places where symbolic descriptions, such as grammars or task rules, meet dynamical systems and neural networks. How do discrete linguistic or cognitive programs show up in continuous neural dynamics? What architectures and learning rules make these representations robust and data-efficient? My broader goal is to develop theory-informed models that connect neurocognitive frameworks—the kinds of computations we care about—with mechanistic accounts of how they are implemented in brains and machines.
